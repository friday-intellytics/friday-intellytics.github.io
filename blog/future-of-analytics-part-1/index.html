Future of Analytics Issue 1 March 13, 2026

Analytics Experience in the AI Era

The three eras of analytics tooling

In 2012, GitHub crossed 3 million users. Developer tooling wasn’t just a category — it was becoming an industry. By 2024, the Developer Experience ecosystem was worth billions. Conferences, companies, entire careers, all built around one idea: make developers more productive and more satisfied.

DevEx, DX — everyone knows the acronym. Everyone invests in it.

Analytics never got an equivalent moment. To understand why, it helps to look at what actually happened. The open question now is whether AI-Assisted Analytics can create Fast Insights without stripping out human judgment.

The SQL era (pre-2010)

Analysts wrote queries, built spreadsheets, emailed reports. Everything was manual and slow. But here’s what’s easy to overlook — the analyst owned the entire chain from question to answer. They understood the data, applied context, and delivered a judgment.

The experience was painful. But it was coherent.

Then the industry decided to improve it — by borrowing.

The dashboard era (2010–2020)

Tableau, Power BI, Looker. The promise was self-serve analytics. Give executives dashboards and they’ll find their own answers. Democratize data.

It was a compelling idea. In practice, something different happened.

Analysts didn’t disappear. They became dashboard factories. The job shifted from answering questions to building artifacts. The analyst’s calendar filled with “can you add a filter to this view” instead of “what should we do about Q3.”

Self-serve moved the bottleneck rather than removing it. From “waiting for the analyst to answer” to “waiting for the analyst to build.” The executive still couldn’t find their own answers. They just had a prettier place to not find them.

Meanwhile, the tooling kept borrowing from developers. SQL editors were code editors with a different syntax highlighter. Data pipelines were CI/CD pipelines, renamed. Notebooks came from coding IDEs. Version control, pull requests, testing frameworks — all developer concepts, retrofitted for data work.

Even the job titles were borrowed. “Analytics engineer” emerged not as its own discipline but as a developer template applied to a fundamentally different craft.

The question that rarely got asked: does any of this fit how analysts actually work?

A developer writes code that compiles or doesn’t. A test passes or fails. The solution space is bounded. The deliverable is objective.

An analyst navigates ambiguity. When someone asks “why did revenue drop in Q3,” the answer could be seasonality, pricing, a competitor move, a data quality issue, or several other things. The analyst’s job is judgment — reading context, weighing evidence, making a call. No test suite validates that. No pipeline automates it.

The tools were good. They just weren’t built for this.

AI-Assisted Analytics in the AI era

Now comes the latest wave. AI agents, copilots, text-to-SQL, autonomous analytics. The pitch: let the executive talk directly to the data. Self-serve, but this time with technology that can actually understand the question.

The technology is genuinely impressive. And yet.

Decisions still require judgment. They require someone who knows that “FISCALWEEKENDSTART_DATE” means the Sunday of the week. Someone who knows that last quarter’s dip was caused by a one-time inventory write-off, not a demand problem. Someone who can say “this number is technically correct but strategically misleading.”

Self-serve was a compelling idea in 2015. It’s still compelling in 2026. But the fundamental challenge hasn’t changed: analytics requires human judgment, and judgment can be assisted but not automated.

Three eras, one pattern

Three eras of analytics. Each brought real progress. Each also optimized for something that, in hindsight, wasn’t quite the right target.

Plot them on two axes — who the analytics was designed for, and what it was designed to deliver — and a pattern becomes visible.

Dashboards for executives — where most organizations operate today. Artifacts delivered to self-serve users. Real value, but often underleveraged. The executive gets a report. The analyst spent two weeks building it. The decision that follows may or may not reference either.

AI agents for executives — where momentum is heading. Answers delivered directly, bypassing the traditional workflow. Faster, certainly. But when the human with context is removed from the loop, the answer’s reliability becomes harder to verify.

Better tools for analysts — where the industry invested heavily. More powerful SQL editors, better notebooks, version-controlled pipelines. Genuine improvements, largely borrowed from the developer ecosystem. Each solves a piece. Few of them connect into a coherent experience.

AI-empowered analysts — the quadrant that hasn’t been built for yet. Where AI assists the analyst rather than routes around them. Where the analyst’s judgment stays central, but AI handles what made analytics so hard in the first place — messy data, ambiguous context, undefined solution spaces. Where the deliverable isn’t an artifact or a raw AI-generated answer, but a decision informed by both data and human expertise.

It’s the data professional’s turn

Every function eventually got its experience moment. Developers got DevEx. Designers got Figma. Salespeople got Salesforce. Product managers got their stack.

Data professionals have waited long enough.

Not for another tool. Not for another dashboard platform. Not for an AI that replaces their judgment. For something built around how they actually work — navigating ambiguity, applying context, and turning data into decisions that move the business.

We think this moment is here. We’re calling it the Analytics Experience — and it starts with AI-Assisted Analytics that helps analysts deliver Fast Insights without giving up ownership of the answer.

Their turn.
